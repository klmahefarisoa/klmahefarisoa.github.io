About Me

I am a One Health advocate and researcher working at the intersection of wildlife conservation, human health, and environmental policy. My research focuses on biodiversity conservation in Madagascar, particularly lemurs, using metagenomic approaches to screen for diseases that could threaten both wildlife and human populations.

My work aims to understand why robust scientific evidence often fails to translate into effective policy action. What are the barriers? How can we better connect research with real-world decision-making? Through my work at KU Leuven, I'm learning to bridge these worlds‚Äîworking with communities, policymakers, NGOs, and fellow researchers to find pathways that connect knowledge with tangible change.

Being a mother while doing this work reminds me daily why it matters. I approach research with both urgency and humility, knowing there's still so much to learn and that meaningful progress requires collaboration across disciplines and perspectives.

The Journey

From forest floors to research halls‚Äîa path shaped by persistence, questions, and a refusal to choose between caring for wildlife and caring for people.

My work began in the forest as a wildlife veterinarian. I could treat injured animals, but I kept watching their habitats disappear while communities struggled alongside the very wildlife I was trying to protect. It became clear these weren't separate problems.

That realization sent me back to school‚Äîtwice‚Äîwhile continuing fieldwork. Two master's degrees later, I was pregnant with my first child when the opportunity came to pursue a PhD at KU Leuven. Moving abroad, starting a doctoral program, becoming a mother‚Äîall at once. People asked if it was too much. But motherhood didn't weaken my resolve; it clarified why this work matters.

Now I focus on a different gap: the space between what science tells us and what policy actually does. We have research showing how to protect biodiversity and support communities, but that knowledge often stops at journal publications. I'm trying to understand why‚Äîand how we can build better pathways from evidence to action.

Along the way, I co-founded Natiora Defenders, Madagascar's first organization dedicated to celebrating and promoting conservation leaders and heroes. We spotlight the individuals and organizations protecting Madagascar's unique biodiversity‚Äîboth marine and terrestrial‚Äîamplifying their voices beyond national borders while engaging local communities in conservation action. The journey hasn't been straightforward, but the most meaningful paths rarely are.
